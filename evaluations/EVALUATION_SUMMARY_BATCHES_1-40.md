## Cross-Batch Patterns and Themes

### Recurring High-Performance Characteristics

**What Makes Propositions Score Well:**
1. **Specific Mechanisms**: Detailed molecular, algorithmic, or causal pathways
2. **Clear Testability**: Concrete predictions with available methodology
3. **Interdisciplinary Integration**: Novel connections between established domains
4. **Practical Applications**: Clear pathways from theory to implementation

**Common Weaknesses:**
1. **Pure Philosophy**: Metaphysics and abstract phenomenology consistently score lower
2. **Incomplete Presentation**: Many propositions end mid-sentence
3. **Speculative Claims**: Extraordinary claims without proportional evidence
4. **Pedagogical Content**: Exposition of known principles rather than novel insights

### Domain Performance Trends

**Consistently High Performers:**
- **Neuroscience**: Detailed mechanistic proposals with clear testability
- **Computer Science**: Novel algorithms and distributed systems design
- **Biology**: Molecular mechanisms with evolutionary context
- **Physics**: Quantum phenomena and materials science
- **Geology**: Specific, testable processes with practical applications

**Moderate Performers:**
- **Linguistics**: Wide variance from highly specific to speculative
- **Political Theory**: Strong institutional analysis, weaker on normative claims
- **Psychology**: Clinical applications stronger than pure theory
- **Anthropology**: Archaeology and material culture excel

**Lower Performers:**
- **Metaphysics**: Consistently lowest - speculative and untestable
- **Philosophy**: Phenomenology and abstract ontology hard to validate
- **Aesthetics**: Interesting frameworks but resist empirical testing
- **Logic**: Variable - formal logic stronger than applied

## Methodological Observations

### Evaluation Consistency

The evaluation process maintained consistent criteria across all 400 propositions:
- **Novel**: Assessed against existing literature in each domain
- **Testable**: Considered available methodology and practical feasibility
- **Significant**: Evaluated potential impact within and across fields
- **Coherent**: Assessed logical structure regardless of content validity

### Notable Challenges

1. **Truncated Propositions**: Many end mid-sentence, requiring inference about complete argument
2. **Domain Expertise**: Evaluating 20+ domains requires breadth over depth
3. **Extraordinary Claims**: Some propositions make remarkable assertions requiring extensive validation
4. **Interdisciplinary Work**: Harder to assess novelty when combining established ideas from different fields

### Meta-Propositions

Several propositions were meta-statements about evaluation constraints rather than substantive claims:
- Batch 022, #8 (Mathematics): Statement about needing complete formal definitions
- Batch 029, #2 (Mathematics): About inability to evaluate tetrakite sequences

These consistently scored low (15-17/150) as they offered no actual content.

## Insights for Proposition Generation

### Success Factors

**To Generate High-Scoring Propositions:**
1. **Specify Mechanisms**: Molecular, computational, or causal detail
2. **Make Predictions**: Testable outcomes with available methods
3. **Show Applications**: Practical implications or uses
4. **Complete Arguments**: Full logical development
5. **Ground in Evidence**: Connect to existing knowledge while extending it

### Common Pitfalls

**To Avoid Low Scores:**
1. Don't make meta-statements about evaluation
2. Avoid pure abstract speculation without empirical grounding
3. Don't simply restate textbook knowledge
4. Avoid extraordinary claims without extraordinary detail
5. Complete the argument - avoid mid-sentence endings
